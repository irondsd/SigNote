# Sign In 🪪

SigNote supports two authentication methods: **Sign-In with Ethereum (SIWE)** for privacy and **Google OAuth** for convenience. Both are built into the sign-in flow, and you can link multiple authentication methods to the same account.

---

## Authentication options

### SIWE — Privacy-focused authentication

**Sign-In with Ethereum (SIWE)** is an open standard for wallet-based authentication. There's no username, no email address, and no password involved.

- **Completely anonymous** — Your Ethereum address is your identity. If you use a clean address, no one can track you.
- **Domain-bound** — SIWE signatures are cryptographically tied to the app domain, preventing phishing attacks.
- **No external account required** — All you need is an Ethereum wallet.

SIWE is ideal if privacy and anonymity are your top concerns.

### Google OAuth — Convenience-focused authentication

**Google OAuth** provides a familiar, quick sign-in experience. If you already have a Google account, signing in takes seconds.

- **Familiar flow** — Uses the standard OAuth 2.0 protocol that you likely know from other apps.
- **One-click sign-in** — No wallet connection, no message signing.
- **Account linking** — You can link both SIWE and Google to the same SigNote account for flexibility.

Google OAuth is ideal if convenience is your priority.

## How SIWE works

### What is SIWE?

SIWE is a simple protocol: instead of proving who you are with a password, you prove it by signing a message with your private key. Only the holder of the private key can produce a valid signature, so a successful sign-in proves you control that wallet address — without ever revealing the key itself.

This is the same cryptographic mechanism that authorises transactions on Ethereum. It's battle-tested and well-understood.

### Sign-in flow

Here's what happens step by step when you sign in with SIWE:

1. **Nonce request** — The app requests a one-time nonce from the server. This prevents replay attacks; each nonce can only be used once.

2. **SIWE message** — A SIWE-formatted message is assembled. It includes the app domain, your wallet address, the nonce, and a timestamp.

3. **Wallet signature** — Your wallet prompts you to sign the message. Your private key never leaves your device; only the signature is sent.

4. **Server verification** — The server verifies:
   - The signature is valid for the claimed address
   - The domain matches the app's configured origin (prevents phishing)
   - The nonce is fresh and hasn't been used before

5. **Session issued** — A JWT session is created and tied to your wallet address. It's valid for 7 days.

### Why SIWE over passwords?

Passwords are weak because people reuse them, forget them, and they can be phished. A wallet signature can't be phished the same way — the message you sign includes the domain, so a signature for `evil.com` won't be accepted by `signote.app`.

### Domain binding security

SIWE messages include the domain of the app. The server **rejects** any signature where the domain or origin doesn't match its configured `NEXTAUTH_URL`. This means a signature obtained on a different site cannot be replayed against SigNote's API.

## How Google OAuth works

### Sign-in flow

When you sign in with Google:

1. You click **"Sign in with Google"**
2. You're redirected to Google's authorization page
3. Google confirms your identity and asks for permission
4. You're redirected back to SigNote with an authorization code
5. SigNote exchanges the code for your Google profile info
6. A session is issued and tied to your account

Your Google password never comes to SigNote — Google handles all authentication.

### Privacy considerations

Signing in with Google means SigNote receives some basic profile information (email, name, profile picture) from Google. If privacy is a concern, consider using SIWE instead.

## Account linking

You can link multiple authentication methods to the same SigNote account. This is useful if:

- You want to sign in with SIWE most of the time but occasionally use Google
- You're migrating from one method to another
- You want a backup way to sign in

### How to link accounts

1. Sign in with your primary method (e.g. SIWE)
2. Go to your profile settings
3. Click **"Link Google account"** (or another method)
4. Complete the OAuth flow
5. Your accounts are now linked — you can sign in with either method

Once linked, both methods access the same account and encrypted notes. Your encryption key material remains tied to your passphrase, not to the authentication method.

### Security implications

Linking accounts does not weaken your encryption:

- Your Secrets and Seals remain encrypted with your passphrase
- The passphrase is never tied to SIWE or Google
- Either authentication method can unlock your notes once you've re-entered your passphrase

However, linking does mean either authentication method can access the account. If one method is compromised, an attacker could sign in as you. Use strong passphrases and keep both your wallet and Google account secure.

---

## What gets cleared on sign-out?

When you sign out:

- Your session token is invalidated
- The Master Encryption Key (MEK) is cleared from memory
- The device share (`deviceShare`) is cleared from `sessionStorage`

This means your encrypted notes are immediately inaccessible until you sign back in and re-enter your passphrase.

Sign-out works the same way regardless of which authentication method you used.

---

## Passphrase vs. sign-in

> 💡 **Note:** The passphrase you set up for [Secrets](/docs/secrets) and [Seals](/docs/seals) is a separate thing — it's used for _encryption_, not for signing in. You can be signed in without having unlocked your encrypted notes.

Your sign-in (SIWE or Google) gives you access to your account and Tier 1 notes. Your passphrase unlocks the encryption for Secrets and Seals. They are independent — changing your sign-in method doesn't affect your encryption.

→ See [Encryption](/docs/encryption) to understand how the passphrase and MEK work separately from authentication.

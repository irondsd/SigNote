# About SigNote ✦

SigNote is a wallet-native notes app with three security tiers. It uses your Ethereum wallet as your identity — no account registration, no username, no password. Just connect your wallet and your notes are yours.

The core idea is simple: **you should own your notes the same way you own your crypto**. Your wallet address is your identity, and only you control what gets encrypted and how strongly.

---

## Three tiers of privacy 🔐

Not every note needs to be a secret. SigNote lets you choose the right privacy level for each piece of information:

| Tier | Name                     | Encryption                   | Best for                                 |
| ---- | ------------------------ | ---------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| 1    | [Notes](/docs/notes)     | None                         | Everyday notes, to-dos, ideas            |
| 2    | [Secrets](/docs/secrets) | AES-GCM, shared session key  | Passwords, private info you access often |
| 3    | [Seals](/docs/seals)     | AES-GCM, unique per-note key | Your most sensitive data                 |

All encryption and decryption happens **in your browser**. The server only ever stores ciphertext — it never sees your plaintext note bodies or any key material capable of decrypting them.

---

## How identity works 🪪

SigNote supports two authentication methods:

- **Sign-In with Ethereum (SIWE)** — Prove ownership of your wallet by signing a message. Perfect for privacy and anonymity.
- **Google OAuth** — Quick, familiar sign-in with your Google account. Perfect for convenience.

Both methods can be linked to the same account, so you have flexibility in how you sign in.

No email required. No passwords. You choose your identity.

→ Learn more in [Sign in](/docs/sign-in)

---

## How encryption works 🔑

For Secrets and Seals, encryption is unlocked with a passphrase you choose during setup. The passphrase is never stored — instead, SigNote derives a **Master Encryption Key (MEK)** by combining a passphrase-derived share with a server-side share. Neither side alone can reconstruct the key.

→ Deep dive in [Encryption](/docs/encryption)
